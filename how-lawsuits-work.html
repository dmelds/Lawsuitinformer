How Lawsuits Work: A Simple Guide

Last updated: March 2026

A lawsuit is a legal process used to resolve disputes between individuals, businesses, or organizations. While the exact steps may vary depending on the case, most lawsuits follow a similar structure.

Important: Lawsuit procedures can vary depending on the type of case and the court system involved. This page provides general educational information and does not constitute legal advice.

1. Filing the Complaint

A lawsuit begins when the person bringing the claim files a legal document called a complaint with the court. This document outlines the allegations and the legal reasons the plaintiff believes the defendant is responsible for harm or damages.

2. The Defendant Responds

The party being sued has an opportunity to respond to the complaint, usually by filing an answer with the court. The response may admit, deny, or challenge the claims made in the complaint.

3. Discovery

Both sides gather evidence through a process called discovery. This stage may include exchanging documents, submitting written questions (interrogatories), and conducting depositions where witnesses answer questions under oath.

4. Settlement Discussions

Many lawsuits are resolved through settlement before reaching trial. Settlement negotiations allow the parties to reach an agreement without continuing the case through the entire court process.

5. Trial

If a settlement is not reached, the case may proceed to trial. During the trial, each side presents evidence and arguments, and a judge or jury decides the outcome.

6. Appeals

In some cases, a party may appeal the court’s decision to a higher court. Appeals typically focus on whether legal errors occurred during the trial rather than re-examining all of the evidence.
