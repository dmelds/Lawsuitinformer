Recent Developments in Transvaginal Mesh Litigation

By David Meldofsky, California-licensed attorney · Founder, Lawsuit Informer

Last updated: March 2026

Transvaginal mesh litigation has changed significantly over time. The largest federal pelvic mesh multidistrict litigations have now been closed, but the broader controversy over mesh complications, product identification, and patient injuries has not disappeared. Many people still research these products because of pain, erosion, repeat surgeries, and questions about whether an implanted device may have been involved.

Important: This page provides general educational information and does not constitute legal advice.

What is transvaginal mesh?

Transvaginal mesh is a surgical mesh product that was used in some procedures involving pelvic organ prolapse or stress urinary incontinence. Over time, some patients reported serious complications, including mesh erosion, pain, infection, bleeding, dyspareunia, urinary problems, and the need for revision surgery. Those complications helped drive major product liability and mass tort litigation involving multiple manufacturers.

What changed in recent years?

One of the most important developments came in 2019, when the FDA ordered the remaining manufacturers of surgical mesh intended for transvaginal repair of pelvic organ prolapse to stop selling and distributing those products in the United States. That action marked a major regulatory shift and remains one of the clearest turning points in the public history of transvaginal mesh litigation.

It is important to be precise about what that meant. The FDA action focused on mesh products intended for transvaginal repair of pelvic organ prolapse, not every mesh-related product in every category. That distinction still matters when readers try to understand what products were affected and why.

What happened to the major federal mesh lawsuits?

The largest federal pelvic mesh multidistrict litigations, often called MDLs, were centralized in the Southern District of West Virginia. Those major MDLs involving manufacturers such as C.R. Bard, American Medical Systems, Boston Scientific, Ethicon, and Coloplast have now been closed.

That does not mean every possible claim everywhere ended at the same time. A better way to understand the current landscape is that the main wave of centralized federal mesh litigation has largely passed, while some issues may still arise in individual cases, state court matters, or other proceedings depending on the facts, the product involved, and the applicable law.

Why do people still research transvaginal mesh lawsuits?

Many people research transvaginal mesh years after implantation because complications may not always be fully understood right away. Some patients later begin investigating whether a specific manufacturer or product family was involved, whether others reported similar injuries, and whether medical records may identify the device used.

In many situations, product identification becomes one of the most important practical issues. That can include the manufacturer name, product name, operative report, implant records, catalog references, and any other documentation that helps connect a person’s procedure to a specific device.

Are there still recent legal developments?

Even though the main federal MDLs are closed, transvaginal mesh remains a live topic in public reporting and legal discussions. Reported settlements and related litigation developments outside the original U.S. MDL structure show that the issue has not simply vanished from public view.

For many readers, the more practical question is not whether the original MDLs still exist, but whether they can identify the product involved and understand how the larger litigation history developed.
