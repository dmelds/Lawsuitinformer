Glyphosate Lawsuits, EPA Labeling, and the Supreme Court

Glyphosate and Roundup lawsuits are back in the national spotlight as pesticide regulation, public-health politics, and product-liability law converge before the U.S. Supreme Court.

Published April 28, 2026 · By David Meldofsky, California-licensed attorney and founder of Lawsuit Informer.

Educational note: This article is for general legal information only. It is not legal advice, does not create an attorney-client relationship, and does not predict the outcome of any specific lawsuit.

Glyphosate, the active ingredient historically associated with Roundup weedkiller, is again at the center of a major legal and public-health debate. The issue is no longer only whether people can sue over alleged cancer risks. It is also whether federal pesticide labeling decisions can limit, or potentially block, state-law failure-to-warn lawsuits.

The renewed attention comes as the U.S. Supreme Court considers a Bayer/Monsanto appeal involving Roundup labeling and as members of the Make America Healthy Again, or MAHA, movement criticize the Trump administration’s support for Bayer’s position in the litigation.

Why glyphosate is back in the news

Glyphosate is one of the most widely discussed herbicide ingredients in the United States. For years, lawsuits have alleged that exposure to Roundup and glyphosate-based products contributed to cancer, particularly non-Hodgkin lymphoma.

Bayer, which acquired Monsanto in 2018, has disputed those allegations and has relied heavily on regulatory determinations, including EPA positions on glyphosate labeling. Plaintiffs, meanwhile, have argued that state-law failure-to-warn claims should be allowed even when a pesticide label has been reviewed under federal law.

That tension is now before the Supreme Court in a case involving whether the Federal Insecticide, Fungicide, and Rodenticide Act, often called FIFRA, preempts certain state-law warning claims involving Roundup.

The Supreme Court issue: federal label approval versus state warning claims

The core legal question is whether a company can be sued under state law for allegedly failing to warn users about a risk when the product’s pesticide label is regulated by EPA under federal law.

Bayer/Monsanto argues that federal pesticide law should prevent state-law failure-to-warn claims that would effectively require a cancer warning not required by EPA. Plaintiffs argue that federal label regulation should not automatically eliminate traditional state-law claims when injured people allege that a manufacturer failed to provide adequate warnings.

In practical terms, the Supreme Court’s decision could shape how future pesticide, toxic-exposure, and product-liability lawsuits are handled when a defendant points to federal agency labeling decisions as a defense.

Why MAHA activists are involved

The issue has also created political tension. NPR reported that some people in the MAHA movement are angry with the Trump administration’s stance on environmental toxins, including its support for the maker of glyphosate. That matters because MAHA supporters often frame pesticides, food additives, and environmental exposures as public-health issues.

For those activists, the Roundup litigation is not just a courtroom dispute. It is part of a larger debate over whether federal agencies, chemical companies, and courts are doing enough to address long-term exposure concerns.

What this could mean for toxic-exposure lawsuits

A Supreme Court ruling in favor of Bayer/Monsanto could strengthen preemption defenses in pesticide labeling cases. That would make it harder for some plaintiffs to bring failure-to-warn claims where the manufacturer argues that EPA-approved labeling controls the issue.

A ruling against Bayer/Monsanto could preserve more room for state-law warning lawsuits, even when a pesticide has gone through federal label review. That would be significant not only for Roundup cases, but potentially for other chemical exposure and product-liability disputes involving federal regulatory schemes.

Either way, the case is important because toxic-exposure lawsuits often sit at the intersection of science, regulation, warnings, corporate conduct, and state tort law.

Why readers should watch this case

The Supreme Court’s decision may affect how courts evaluate future claims involving pesticides and other regulated products. It may also influence settlement strategy, litigation risk, and how companies argue that federal agency decisions should limit state-law lawsuits.

For people following Roundup lawsuits, the case is worth watching because it could affect the legal framework behind many failure-to-warn claims. For people following environmental health issues, it is also a reminder that regulatory approval and civil liability are often separate but overlapping questions.
