Transvaginal Mesh Lawsuits

By David Meldofsky, California-licensed attorney · Founder, Lawsuit Informer

Last updated: March 2026

Transvaginal mesh lawsuits have involved allegations that certain pelvic mesh products caused serious complications, including erosion, pain, infection, urinary problems, sexual dysfunction, and the need for revision surgery. This page serves as a starting point for readers who want to understand the general litigation history, the current status of major mesh cases, and how public product-identification records may help connect a procedure to a manufacturer or product family.

Important: This page provides general educational information and does not constitute legal advice.

What is transvaginal mesh?

Transvaginal mesh refers to certain surgical mesh products used in procedures involving pelvic organ prolapse or stress urinary incontinence. Over time, many patients reported complications that led to safety concerns, FDA action, and large-scale product liability litigation.

Why did transvaginal mesh lawsuits grow so large?

These lawsuits grew in part because many claims involved similar allegations against major manufacturers, including claims involving product design, warnings, complications after implantation, and repeat surgeries. Large numbers of federal cases were centralized in pelvic mesh multidistrict litigations, often called MDLs.

What is the current status of transvaginal mesh litigation?

The largest federal pelvic mesh MDLs were centralized in the Southern District of West Virginia, and the major proceedings involving Bard, AMS, Boston Scientific, Ethicon, and Coloplast are listed by the court as closed. That does not mean every issue connected to transvaginal mesh disappeared, but it does mean the main federal MDL phase has ended.

What did the FDA do?

The FDA states that in 2019 it ordered manufacturers to stop selling mesh devices for transvaginal repair of pelvic organ prolapse in the United States. FDA materials also continue to explain that women who already received these products should continue routine follow-up care and discuss symptoms or complications with their healthcare providers.

Why product identification matters

In many mesh cases, one of the most important questions is which product was actually implanted. Public FDA records may help identify a manufacturer, product family, 510(k) reference, or some catalog or recall details. But exact identification often depends on medical records such as operative reports, implant stickers, hospital records, and revision surgery documentation.
