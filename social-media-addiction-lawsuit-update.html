Social Media Addiction Lawsuit Update

Updated March 28, 2026

Social media addiction litigation has moved into a more serious phase in 2026. A California jury recently found Meta and YouTube liable in a closely watched case, while the federal multidistrict litigation continues moving toward trial.

What happened this week?

One of the biggest developments came on March 25, 2026, when a Los Angeles jury found Meta and YouTube liable in a landmark lawsuit involving allegations that social media platforms were designed in ways that harmed a young user. Reports indicate the jury awarded $3 million in compensatory damages and later recommended an additional $3 million in punitive damages, though the judge still has authority over the final damages award.

The case drew national attention because it is being viewed as an early test of whether plaintiffs can hold social media companies responsible not just for content on the platforms, but for design features that allegedly encourage compulsive use, prolonged engagement, and addiction-like behavior in minors.

Why this verdict matters

This was not just another lawsuit filing. It was a jury verdict. That matters because verdicts can influence how companies, courts, and plaintiffs evaluate similar claims going forward. The case also appears to strengthen the broader theory that lawsuits may focus on platform design, including features such as endless feeds, autoplay, notifications, and other engagement systems, instead of trying to hold platforms liable for every third-party post.

Reports also noted that TikTok and Snap settled out of the California case before trial, leaving Meta and YouTube as the remaining defendants in front of the jury.

What is happening in the federal social media addiction litigation?

Separate from that California case, a large federal multidistrict litigation, often called an MDL, is pending in the Northern District of California before Judge Yvonne Gonzalez Rogers. The litigation groups together many lawsuits raising similar allegations involving youth mental health harms and allegedly addictive platform design.

According to the Judicial Panel on Multidistrict Litigation statistics report dated March 2, 2026, MDL No. 3047 had 2,407 pending actions at that time. Court records also show a jury trial has been set for July 17, 2026, signaling that the litigation is continuing to move forward rather than stalling out.

What are these lawsuits alleging?

In broad terms, social media addiction lawsuits allege that certain platforms were designed to maximize screen time and repeated use, especially among adolescents, while companies knew or should have known about the potential mental health risks.

Plaintiffs have raised allegations involving anxiety, depression, eating disorder-related harms, sleep disruption, compulsive use, and other serious consequences allegedly tied to prolonged use. Individual cases can differ significantly, and outcomes depend on the evidence in each claim.

What could happen next?

The recent verdict does not mean every case will succeed, and appeals are likely. But it does suggest these lawsuits are moving beyond early procedural battles and into a stage where verdicts, settlements, and bellwether trials could shape the value and direction of the litigation.

The next major thing to watch is how courts handle additional test cases and whether more defendants choose to settle rather than risk trial.
