Social Media Addiction Lawsuit Update

By David Meldofsky, California-licensed attorney · Founder, Lawsuit Informer

Last updated: March 2026

Social media addiction litigation has entered a more serious stage. A closely watched California trial recently ended with a jury finding Meta and YouTube liable, while thousands of related claims continue moving forward in federal court. For families researching these lawsuits, the latest developments suggest that the litigation is becoming more active and more closely watched nationwide.

Important: This page provides general educational information and does not constitute legal advice.

What is happening in the social media addiction lawsuits?

Social media addiction lawsuits generally allege that certain platforms were designed to maximize engagement in ways that encouraged compulsive use, especially among children and teens. Plaintiffs claim that features such as endless scrolling, algorithmic recommendations, autoplay, and persistent notifications contributed to harmful patterns of use and worsened serious mental health problems.

These lawsuits do not all look exactly the same, but many focus on allegations involving depression, anxiety, body image issues, disordered eating, sleep disruption, self-harm-related risks, and other harms families say were linked to prolonged and unhealthy platform use.

What was the recent California verdict?

In March 2026, a Los Angeles jury found Meta and YouTube liable in a major social media addiction case involving a young plaintiff. Reports indicate the jury awarded $3 million in compensatory damages and later recommended an additional $3 million in punitive damages, subject to final court review.

The case drew national attention because it tested whether plaintiffs could successfully argue that social media companies should be held responsible for allegedly addictive product design rather than simply for third-party content posted on their platforms.

Why the verdict matters

This was important because it was not just another complaint being filed. It was a jury verdict. That gives the litigation a different level of attention and may affect how future cases, settlement discussions, and trial strategies develop.

Reports also stated that TikTok and Snap settled before the trial began, leaving Meta and YouTube as the remaining defendants in front of the jury. That detail matters because it shows some defendants may try to avoid the uncertainty of a public trial.

What is the federal MDL?

Separate from the California state court case, many social media addiction lawsuits have been consolidated in a federal multidistrict litigation known as MDL No. 3047, pending in the Northern District of California. MDLs are used to coordinate pretrial proceedings when many lawsuits raise similar allegations.

As of the Judicial Panel on Multidistrict Litigation statistics report dated March 2, 2026, MDL 3047 had 2,407 pending actions. That number helps show the scale of the litigation and why the recent verdict has attracted so much interest.

Why people connect social media use to lawsuits

Families and individuals often begin researching legal claims after noticing significant emotional, behavioral, or psychological changes that they believe may have been worsened by excessive platform use. In many cases, the concern is not simply screen time itself, but whether certain products were intentionally designed to increase dependency and keep young users engaged despite known risks.

  • Compulsive or excessive platform use
  • Depression or anxiety symptoms
  • Body image or eating-disorder-related concerns
  • Sleep disruption and social withdrawal
  • Concerns about design features aimed at repeated engagement
