What Is a Demand Letter?

By David Meldofsky, California-licensed attorney · Founder, Lawsuit Informer

Last updated: March 2026

A demand letter is a written communication that usually explains a dispute, describes the harm or problem involved, and asks the other side to take some action. In some situations, it may request payment, correction of a problem, preservation of evidence, or another response before a lawsuit is filed.

Important: This page provides general educational information and does not constitute legal advice.

What is a demand letter?

A demand letter is often a formal written notice sent before or during a dispute. It usually explains what happened, why the sender believes they have a legal claim or complaint, and what they are asking the other side to do in response.

Not every dispute involves a demand letter, and not every demand letter leads to a lawsuit. But in many situations, it serves as an early step in trying to resolve a problem or clarify each side’s position.

What can a demand letter include?

  • A summary of the facts
  • A description of the harm, loss, or dispute
  • A request for payment, action, or correction
  • A deadline for responding
  • References to documents, records, or other supporting information
  • A statement that further action may follow if the issue is not resolved

Why people send demand letters

In some cases, a demand letter is used to open settlement discussions or encourage resolution without immediate litigation. In others, it helps create a record showing that the issue was raised and that the other side had notice of the complaint.

A demand letter can also help organize the issues. By putting the facts, timeline, and requested outcome in writing, the parties may better understand where the dispute stands.

Does sending a demand letter mean a lawsuit has been filed?

No. A demand letter is not the same thing as filing a lawsuit. It is usually a pre-litigation or early dispute step. Sometimes the matter resolves after a demand letter. Sometimes it does not. Whether a case proceeds further depends on the facts, the response, the damages involved, and other legal considerations.

Who sends a demand letter?

In some situations, a lawyer may send the letter on behalf of a client. In other situations, a person or business may send one directly. The effect of a demand letter can vary depending on the type of case, the wording used, the documentation available, and the legal issues involved.

What kinds of disputes may involve demand letters?

Demand letters can appear in many different contexts, including personal injury disputes, contract issues, property damage matters, consumer problems, business disputes, and other civil claims. The content and tone may differ depending on the type of dispute.

What happens after a demand letter is sent?

The other side may respond, deny the claim, ask for more information, make a settlement offer, ignore the letter, or send the matter to an insurer or lawyer for review. In some cases, more negotiation follows. In others, the dispute continues.

Why wording and timing can matter

Demand letters can have legal and strategic importance. The wording used, the documents attached, and the timing of the letter may all matter. In some disputes, a letter that is too vague, too aggressive, or poorly timed may be less effective than a clear and organized one.

Timing can matter for another reason too: sending a demand letter does not necessarily stop legal deadlines from running. That is one reason people should be careful about relying on general assumptions about timing.

What people often gather before a demand letter

  • A written timeline of events
  • Contracts, receipts, invoices, or billing records
  • Photos, videos, or screenshots
  • Medical records or repair records where relevant
  • Emails, texts, or other communications
  • Names of witnesses or involved parties
