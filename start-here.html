Thinking About a Lawsuit? A Beginner's Guide

By David Meldofsky, California-licensed attorney · Founder, Lawsuit Informer

Last updated: April 21, 2026

Lawsuit topics can feel overwhelming — unfamiliar terms, case types, and categories all at once. This guide walks through the basics in order: how lawsuits work, what happens after you contact a lawyer, what evidence tends to matter, how long cases take, and what to ask before you sign anything.

Step 1: Learn How Lawsuits Work

A strong first step is understanding the basic structure of a lawsuit: how a claim begins, what lawyers typically review early on, and how a case moves through filing, discovery, settlement discussions, and — in some matters — trial. Getting this overview first makes every later step easier to follow.

It also helps to review common lawsuit mistakes — missed deadlines, lost records, and communication problems — that tend to cause trouble later if they are not handled early.

Step 2: Know What Happens After You Contact a Lawyer

Most firms begin with an intake conversation, ask follow-up questions, and request records relevant to the potential claim. After reviewing that information, the firm decides whether the matter fits the type of case they handle. That decision is not a judgment of your situation overall — it is about whether the facts line up with the specific type of case the firm is investigating.

Step 3: Understand What Evidence Helps

What counts as useful information varies by case type, but common examples include medical records, employment history, product purchase records, photos, messages, and a written timeline of what happened and when. Even partial records can be useful — it is usually better to share what you have than to wait until everything is perfectly organized.

Step 4: Get a Realistic Sense of Timing

How long a case takes is one of the first questions most people ask. Some matters resolve in months; others take several years. The timeline depends on the facts, the court, the number of parties involved, whether early motions narrow the issues, and whether the case settles before trial or continues through discovery, expert reports, and a verdict.

Mass tort cases that involve large numbers of plaintiffs often move on a slower schedule than individual claims, because coordinated proceedings have their own pacing. Knowing that upfront makes the process much less frustrating as it unfolds.

Step 5: Know What to Ask Before Signing

Before agreeing to representation, it is worth understanding the fee structure, communication expectations, and next steps. Practical questions to ask include:

  • How does the firm charge — contingency, hourly, or flat fee?
  • Who will handle my case day-to-day?
  • How often will I receive updates, and through what channel?
  • What records or information do you need from me first?
  • What are the likely next steps, and on what timeline?

Asking these questions upfront avoids surprises later and gives you a clearer picture of whether the firm is the right fit.

Frequently Asked Questions

What should I read first if I am new to lawsuits?

Start with How Lawsuits Work to understand the basic structure — filing, discovery, settlement discussions, and trial. From there, What Happens After You Contact a Lawyer covers the intake process most people go through next.

What happens after I contact a lawyer?

Most firms begin with an intake conversation, ask follow-up questions, and request records relevant to the claim. After reviewing the information, the firm decides whether the matter fits the type of case they handle.

What kinds of records or information may matter?

It depends on the claim, but common examples include medical records, employment history, product purchase records, photos, messages, and a written timeline of what happened and when.

How long can a lawsuit take?

Some matters resolve in months; others take several years. Timelines depend on the facts, the court, the number of parties, and whether the case settles early or proceeds through discovery and trial.

What is the difference between a mass tort and a class action?

In a class action, a single lawsuit covers everyone in the group and any recovery is divided among class members. In a mass tort, each person has an individual case, even when many similar cases are coordinated together.
