States Suing AI Companies

By David Meldofsky, California-licensed attorney · Founder, Lawsuit Informer

Last updated: June 6, 2026

Alongside the private lawsuits filed by individuals and families, a growing number of state attorneys general and other government authorities have taken direct action against AI companies. This page is a running overview of who has acted, against which company, and on what basis. Because this area moves quickly, it is updated as new actions are reported.

How to use this page:

This is a general, educational overview of government action — not a complete or official docket. Government enforcement actions are separate from private claims. If you were personally harmed, see the linked pages for how individual claims work.

Why States Are Acting

With no comprehensive federal AI law in place, much of the legal pressure on AI companies has come from the states. State attorneys general have used existing tools — consumer-protection statutes, deceptive-trade-practice laws, child-safety authority, and even professional licensing rules — to address conduct those laws' drafters never specifically anticipated. The result is a patchwork of state enforcement actions rather than a single national approach.

Action Involving OpenAI

A state filed suit against OpenAI and its chief executive, alleging the company failed to warn users that its chatbot could be dangerous and marketed it as safe, including for children. The action raised product-liability, negligence, and deceptive-trade-practice theories and was reported as the first state suit of its kind against the company.

For the broader OpenAI litigation, including the private cases, see OpenAI Lawsuits.

Action Involving Character.AI

Character.AI has faced more than one state action. One state attorney general filed suit alleging the platform's chatbots preyed on children and led them toward self-harm. Separately, another state brought an enforcement action alleging that a chatbot posed as a licensed medical professional in violation of its medical practice law — described as the first U.S. action focused specifically on chatbots impersonating medical professionals.

For more detail, see Character.AI Lawsuits.

Action Involving Grok and xAI

Government scrutiny of xAI's Grok tool has been significant. A state attorney general opened an investigation and issued a cease-and-desist order over the tool's generation of nonconsensual sexualized images, and a major U.S. city sued under its consumer-protection ordinance. Regulators in several other countries also opened investigations or imposed restrictions.

For more detail, see Grok and xAI Lawsuits and the related TAKE IT DOWN Act explainer.

Multistate and Coalition Action

Beyond individual states, a coalition of dozens of state attorneys general signed a joint letter raising concerns about AI-generated nonconsensual intimate images of real people, including minors. Coalition letters of this kind often signal coordinated interest and can precede further action. Reporting has also described a large volume of chatbot-specific safety bills introduced across many states, indicating that legislative activity is running alongside enforcement.

Enforcement Actions vs. Private Claims

It is worth being clear about the difference between the two kinds of legal action:

  • Government enforcement actions are brought by a state attorney general, a city, or another authority. They typically seek to change company conduct, stop a practice, or impose civil penalties. Individuals do not enroll in them.
  • Private claims are brought by individuals or families who allege they were personally harmed, and typically seek compensation. These follow a different path and depend on the specific facts and the applicable law.

Both can arise from the same underlying conduct. If you were personally affected, a private claim may be possible regardless of whether a government action exists.

What to Watch Next

Expect this area to keep developing: more state attorneys general may bring actions, the novel professional-licensing and consumer-protection theories will be tested in court, the multistate coalition may take further steps, and state legislatures may pass AI-specific laws that change the landscape. This page is updated as those develop.

Common Questions

Which states have taken action?

Reported examples include a Florida suit against OpenAI, Kentucky and Pennsylvania actions against Character.AI, a Utah action involving an AI chatbot, a California investigation and cease-and-desist involving Grok, and a multistate letter signed by dozens of attorneys general. A U.S. city has also sued.

Are these the same as private lawsuits?

No. These are government enforcement actions, separate from private claims by individuals or families, though both can arise from the same conduct.

Can I join a state's action?

Generally no — government actions are not something individuals enroll in. If you were personally harmed, a separate private claim may be possible. A case review can help clarify.
