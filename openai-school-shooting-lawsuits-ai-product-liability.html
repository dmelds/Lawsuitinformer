OpenAI Faces School Shooting Lawsuits: AI Liability Questions

By David Meldofsky

Published May 2, 2026 · Updated May 10, 2026

The seven federal lawsuits filed against OpenAI in San Francisco after the February 2026 Tumbler Ridge school shooting will likely be remembered for the question they force courts to answer first: is generative AI output a product, or is it content?

That framing question matters more than any individual fact in any of the seven complaints. If courts treat ChatGPT output as a product — defective in design, manufacture, or warning — existing tort doctrine fits relatively cleanly, and plaintiffs have a path. If courts treat the output as content created in response to user inputs, then Section 230 of the Communications Decency Act becomes the central battleground, and a 1996 statute written to protect early-internet message boards becomes the backbone of liability rules for systems that didn't exist when it was drafted.

Garcia v. Character Technologies showed last year that at least one federal court is willing to entertain the product framing. The OpenAI cases will test whether that signal becomes a pattern.

Important note

This article is general educational commentary, not legal advice. It does not evaluate the merits of any lawsuit, predict outcomes, or create an attorney-client relationship. Nothing has been proven against OpenAI; the lawsuits are at an early stage.

What the lawsuits claim

The lawsuits arise from the February 10, 2026 shooting in Tumbler Ridge, British Columbia. Reuters reported that seven federal lawsuits were filed in San Francisco by families affected by the shooting, with the Associated Press noting claims that include negligence, wrongful death, and product liability theories.

The complaints, citing earlier Wall Street Journal reporting, allege that OpenAI's internal safety team flagged the user's conversations and recommended notifying police months before the attack, but that recommendation wasn't acted on. They also allege a specific product-design failure: after one account was disabled, the user created another and continued using ChatGPT.

That second allegation matters. Most "AI failed to monitor users" theories are diffuse and run into Section 230 problems immediately. An allegation that the account-enforcement system was circumventable is narrower and more concrete — it's about how the system was built and operated, not about what any specific output said. That's the framing most likely to survive a motion to dismiss.

Plaintiffs' counsel Jay Edelson is among the more sophisticated tech plaintiffs' lawyers in the country and has signaled the lawsuits are part of a broader effort. That doesn't speak to the merits, but it suggests these cases are being structured for staying power, not quick settlement.

The three questions courts will have to answer

The doctrinal questions these lawsuits force are, until now, mostly theoretical. Three are central:

  • Whether a chatbot is a product, a service, or expressive technology — and which body of law applies as a result.
  • Whether Section 230, designed for platforms hosting third-party content, fits a system that generates output rather than hosting it.
  • Whether companies operating AI systems can have a legal duty to warn authorities when their own systems flag a credible threat — a question courts have generally answered "no" for product manufacturers but haven't squarely addressed for AI companies with internal threat-detection systems.

How courts answer the first question largely determines the answers to the second and third. If a chatbot is a product, traditional product-liability doctrine applies and Section 230 becomes irrelevant. If it's content, the analysis runs the other way.

Product liability is the strongest framing

Product liability has historically applied to physical goods that cause physical injury. Software has fit awkwardly into that framework for decades. AI fits more awkwardly still, because the "defect" in question often isn't a manufacturing flaw or a missing warning — it's the system's behavior in response to inputs the manufacturer didn't write.

The strongest framing in the OpenAI complaints isn't "ChatGPT generated dangerous content." It's that OpenAI's account-enforcement, escalation, and threat-response systems were inadequate to the risks the company itself had identified internally. That's a process-and-design claim, not a content claim. It mirrors how courts have analyzed product-liability claims against manufacturers whose internal records showed they knew about a problem and didn't address it — a familiar legal pattern in pharmaceutical and consumer-product cases.

Garcia v. Character Technologies is the early roadmap

Garcia v. Character Technologies, decided at the motion-to-dismiss stage in the Middle District of Florida, declined to dismiss product-liability-style claims against the company behind Character.AI on First Amendment and Section 230 grounds. Critically, the court treated the chatbot's output as something other than purely third-party content — meaning Section 230's bedrock distinction (platform versus publisher of someone else's words) didn't cleanly apply.

Garcia isn't binding on the courts hearing the OpenAI cases. But it is a federal opinion analyzing the same defenses OpenAI will raise, and it concluded those defenses don't automatically dispose of AI chatbot claims at the pleadings stage. Plaintiffs will lean on it heavily.

Section 230 is a weaker defense than it looks

Section 230(c)(1) protects online services from being treated as the publisher or speaker of information provided by "another information content provider." That language was written in 1996 with bulletin boards, comment sections, and forwarded email in mind. It assumes a clean distinction between the platform and the speaker.

Generative AI breaks that distinction. When ChatGPT generates a response, the user provides the prompt, but the language, structure, and substance of the output come from OpenAI's model. Whether that output qualifies as "information provided by another information content provider" is genuinely unclear — and the most natural reading of the statute suggests it doesn't, because no third-party content provider is supplying the language.

OpenAI will likely argue ChatGPT outputs are functionally akin to user content because they emerge in response to user prompts. That argument has some force, but it requires treating the model as a passive conduit for user intent — and the marketing, the engineering, and OpenAI's own descriptions of its systems all describe ChatGPT as something far more active than a conduit.

In my view, Section 230 is a weak defense to product-design claims of the type pleaded here. It may apply to narrower theories framed as "what the chatbot said," but the strongest claims in the OpenAI complaints aren't framed that way. Garcia signals courts are starting to recognize the mismatch.

Duty to warn is the weakest theory but the most rhetorically effective

Tarasoff v. Regents of the University of California established a duty for therapists to warn identifiable potential victims under specific circumstances, and it required a special relationship — the therapist-patient bond — to trigger the duty. Courts have generally declined to extend Tarasoff to product manufacturers, software developers, or platforms with millions of users, because no analogous special relationship exists.

Plaintiffs' best argument is that OpenAI's internal escalation system, having identified this specific user as a credible threat, created something functionally analogous to a special relationship: unique knowledge plus operational capacity to act. That's a serious argument, not a frivolous one. But no court has held that a product manufacturer's internal threat-detection records create a Tarasoff-style duty, and asking a court to be the first is a heavy doctrinal lift.

The duty-to-warn theory is most useful to plaintiffs as rhetorical leverage during discovery. The more internal documents show OpenAI knew about and discussed the user's behavior, the more uncomfortable the company's litigation position becomes — regardless of whether the duty-to-warn claim itself survives.

The First Amendment defense follows the framing

OpenAI may also argue ChatGPT outputs are protected expression. The argument has a doctrinal pedigree but a difficult application: there is no settled First Amendment doctrine treating algorithmically generated text as protected speech of the company that built the system. Courts will likely be reluctant to create that doctrine in the context of a school-shooting case.

Plaintiffs' response will be that they aren't suing over protected ideas or expression at all. They're suing over product design, escalation failures, and account-enforcement gaps. The First Amendment analysis follows the product-versus-content framing question — if the claims are read as product defects, the First Amendment defense weakens substantially.

Florida investigation adds broader context

The Tumbler Ridge lawsuits aren't isolated. Florida Attorney General James Uthmeier announced a criminal investigation into OpenAI and ChatGPT related to a separate Florida State University shooting. Criminal investigations operate under different standards from civil claims and don't establish wrongdoing. But the convergence — civil lawsuits, state criminal scrutiny, and prior regulatory attention — signals that AI safety questions have moved out of academic debate.

What courts will likely struggle with

A few things worth watching that don't fit neatly into the public framing of the cases:

Causation will probably be harder than any of the doctrinal questions. Proving ChatGPT contributed to or caused the shooting — as opposed to being one element in a complex set of human and contextual factors — is the kind of evidentiary problem that has sunk product-liability claims in less novel contexts. Even if the doctrinal questions resolve in plaintiffs' favor, causation may be where the cases actually get decided.

Courts may avoid the First Amendment question by ruling narrowly on other grounds. Federal courts generally prefer to dispose of cases on the narrowest available theory. If a court can decide the early motions on Section 230 or product-liability classification grounds, it likely will, leaving the First Amendment doctrine for another day. That preference helps plaintiffs in the short term and creates uncertainty for AI companies looking for clear rules.

The discovery record is the wild card. The complaints rely on Wall Street Journal reporting that OpenAI's internal safety team flagged the user and recommended police notification. If discovery confirms that record — internal communications, escalation memos, threat-detection logs — the cases become substantially stronger and OpenAI's settlement calculus shifts. If discovery shows a more ambiguous internal record, the doctrinal arguments become academic.

Whether these cases consolidate matters more than people think. Edelson's involvement and the structure of the filings suggest plaintiffs may push for some form of coordination, possibly an MDL down the line if AI-harm cases proliferate. Coordination changes settlement leverage. Watch for it.

What this means for other AI companies

The outcome of the OpenAI lawsuits will shape the legal exposure of every company building or deploying AI systems with public-facing user interaction. If product-liability and process-failure framings survive early dismissal, AI developers, chatbot platforms, and the companies integrating those systems into products will face pressure to demonstrate not just technical safety, but legally defensible safety operations: documented escalation procedures, clear account-enforcement protocols, retained internal safety reviews.

The standard isn't going to be whether a company has a safety team. It's going to be what the safety team did with what it knew.

What to watch next

The next major filings will be motions to dismiss, where OpenAI will argue plaintiffs haven't established a legally cognizable duty, sufficient causation, or a viable product-liability theory in the face of Section 230 and First Amendment defenses. If any claims survive, discovery will likely focus on internal safety reviews, escalation policies, account-enforcement records, threat-detection system outputs, and what OpenAI personnel knew before February 10, 2026.

Bottom line

These cases will probably be decided on procedural grounds long before any jury sees them — that's how most genuinely novel-tort cases get resolved. But the doctrinal fights over Section 230, the First Amendment, and product-liability classification will shape settlement leverage even if no claim ever reaches trial. Which means the early motions matter more than the eventual outcome, and the framing battle — product versus content — is the one to watch.
